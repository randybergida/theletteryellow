The Letter Yellow was born in the winter of 2011, emerging from a period of significant personal and creative transition for songwriter Randy Bergida. Following the conclusion of his previous band, Skidmore Fountain, and the release of his 2009 solo record, Randy sought to channel that creative energy into a project built on a foundation of raw, immediate collaboration. The result was a 12-track debut album, Walking Down the Streets, which struck a perfect pace of blues and rock—all with a modern kick, a strong beat, and lyrics that were both offbeat and universal. This Brooklyn-made album established the foundation of The Letter Yellow and introduced the band's signature analog style.

Since the band’s inception, founding members Randy Bergida, Abe Pollack, and Mike Thies remained committed to an authentic sound: real musicians playing together in a real space, making real music. A major catalyst for this approach was Nolan Thies, who served as the engineer and mixer for the band's second album, Watercolor Overcast (2015). Nolan was instrumental in that record, acting as a crucial creative partner who was 100% on board to push for a truly analog process. Together, the group committed to 10 songs, a beloved Neve console, and a 24-track tape machine, creating a record that never touched a computer. By removing the possibility of "fixing it later," they were forced to be present and capture the "magic take" in real-time.

The project reached a pivotal moment during the band’s Living Room residency, just after the release of Watercolor Overcast, when Abe Pollack decided to leave to focus on his own creative pursuits. While Randy initially felt this might be the end of the road, Mike Thies was undeterred. He called his brother, Nolan, to join the lineup, cementing a new creative partnership that would define the band’s future.

With Nolan now fully integrated into the fold, the band entered a new phase of production. This evolution culminated in the 2018 release, On The Other Side. Driven by a continued love for analog gear and the immediacy of tape, the band set out to make a record that met their core ideals. They secured a studio to record for 18 hours straight, then produced the remainder of the record in their own production studios over the course of a year. This approach allowed them to craft the music exactly as they wanted, without the limitations of being on the clock. The result is a groove and vibe-driven record that defines the current legacy of The Letter Yellow.
